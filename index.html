The project responds to a request for a pavilion containing public restrooms. Conceived as both a civic utility and a sensory device, it transforms a simple programme into an immersive spatial experience. A forest of ropes structures the pavilion as a tactile and visual field, inviting visitors to slow down, move through layers of filters and rediscover space through perception rather than enclosure. Water flows down the exterior walls to preserve privacy and soften sound from within. Circular elements echo the geometry of the ropes, while the white materiality amplifies the colours of the surrounding city.

This project investigates matter as a generator of space rather than a passive surface. Through experimental models, architecture emerges from texture, erosion, density and natural transformation. The proposal studies how mineral and organic behaviour can create spatial atmospheres. Cavities, thickness, light penetration and irregular geometries become tools for design. Instead of imposing form onto material, the process allows material logic itself to shape architecture. The result is a primitive and contemporary spatial language rooted in nature.

Academic Project

Conceived as a long architectural threshold, the project positions itself at the edge of the site to create a clear separation between two contrasting worlds: the boulevard and the park. The building acts as a protective barrier, preserving the open landscape behind it while offering a calmer and more intimate public environment. Its curved geometry responds to the urban condition of the plot and to the formal language of the surrounding context. The project combines a cultural and sports programme within a continuous structural framework that wraps the public space and integrates the metro access into the composition. A glazed ground floor establishes visual continuity between inside and outside, encouraging movement, pause and public appropriation. The architecture becomes both infrastructure and civic space: a building that protects, connects and activates the edge between city and park.

Born from a territorial reading of the Morlanwelz–Binche landscape, the project investigates the progressive fragmentation of agricultural land caused by twentieth-century suburban expansion. Former continuous productive grounds have been reduced into isolated residual plots, weakening both ecological continuity and local food production.

The proposal reactivates this condition through the creation of a peri-urban agricultural park positioned between two existing urban poles. Rather than freezing the landscape, the intervention establishes a productive framework where cultivation, housing, hospitality and collective life coexist. A system of parallel bands organizes the site into alternating zones of production and communal programs. Perpendicular crossings introduce hierarchy, separating public sequences from private domestic areas while maintaining permeability across the ensemble. A continuous elevated promenade stitches the entire composition together. Acting simultaneously as circulation device, belvedere and architectural identity, it links fields, dwellings, workshops and public spaces into a coherent territorial structure. The project explores how architecture can mediate between city and countryside, transforming leftover land into a resilient landscape of production, habitation and shared use.

Landscape Urbanism

Morlanwelz–Binche, Belgium

Academic Project

Located in Kuşdili Çayırı, in the heart of Kadıköy, this project begins with a reading of the large open and underused spaces surrounding Kurbağalıdere. The proposal seeks to reactivate this urban edge by transforming it into a generous public landscape where sport, movement, rest and daily life can coexist. The intervention is conceived as a dynamic screen placed between the city and the park. Rather than acting as a barrier in a closed sense, the building filters, frames and protects the green space while establishing a new public facade towards the urban side. Its linear organization creates a clear threshold: the city is addressed on the south, while the sports fields and open landscape unfold behind. A continuous public circulation runs along the southern facade, allowing visitors to move through the building at all times of the day. From this elevated and inhabited edge, the project offers views towards sport, vegetation and collective activity. The architecture becomes an active interface: a civic machine where public life and athletic practice remain connected without ever disturbing one another. Through this relationship between openness, program and movement, Ecran Dynamique proposes a new intensity for the site — one that reinforces the identity of Kuşdili Çayırı as both an urban park and a place of shared, everyday vitality.

MELIK SIMSEK

Melik Simsek is an architecture student whose work explores the relationship between landscape, structure and public life. His approach is grounded in a strong interest in composition, spatial perception and the way architecture can guide movement through sequences of views, thresholds and changes in level. His projects often begin with a careful reading of the site, paying particular attention to topography, circulation and the dialogue between built form and open ground. Through drawings, models and spatial analysis, he seeks to develop proposals that are both rigorous in their organisation and open in their use. During his academic path, he has developed a particular interest in public architecture, landscape strategies and the articulation between city and terrain. He sees architecture not as an isolated object, but as a framework capable of revealing relationships between climate, ground, body and collective space. Several of his recent projects investigate sloping sites, urban edges and places where the section becomes the principal design tool. In these contexts, differences in level are treated not as obstacles, but as opportunities to construct sequence, orientation and identity.

His design method combines analytical observation with conceptual clarity. Each project is developed through a process that links context, programme and atmosphere, with particular attention to how spatial order can generate both legibility and intensity.

Beyond architecture alone, his interests extend to landscape urbanism, territorial questions and the role of public space in shaping everyday experience. He is especially attentive to the capacity of architecture to organise visual continuity, rhythm and urban presence within complex environments.

His current research also reflects a growing interest in the relationship between architecture and the ground: excavation, platforms, retaining systems and layered transitions are explored as ways of constructing both form and experience. In this sense, the section is not only a technical drawing, but a conceptual instrument at the core of the project.

Through academic work, competitions and long-term investigation, Melik Simsek aims to develop an architectural language that is both disciplined and sensitive: a language capable of engaging landscape, programme and urban life with precision, coherence and contemporary relevance.
